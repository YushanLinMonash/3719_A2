Australia Energy Consumption

Visual analysis of energy consumption data in Australia

Electricity Energy Consumption by State

The data show Australia's energy consumption from 2000 to 2004, with a gradual increase over time. In 2000, the total consumption was 5011.8 PJ, rising to 5399.2 PJ by 2004. The highest growth occurred between 2002 and 2003, where consumption jumped from 5138.67 PJ to 5284.73 PJ.

Energy Consumption by Type

This stacked bar chart displays Australia's total energy consumption and derived production over time. In 2000, consumption was 22,676.9 PJ, and production was 3,395.7 PJ. This gap continued to widen in later years, with consumption growing to 23,746.23 PJ in 2002 while production slightly decreased.

Energy Consumption by Industry and State

The data show energy consumption across industries in Australia in 2000. Electricity generation consumed the most energy (1507.9 PJ), followed by manufacturing (1188.3 PJ). Agriculture and construction used significantly less, at 85.5 PJ and 28.2 PJ, respectively. Commercial services consumed 214.5 PJ, making it a moderate contributor.

Energy Consumption Comparison by Industry

The radar chart shows electricity consumption by industry from 2016, with the electricity sector consuming the most (227,617.16 GWh), followed by mining (11,985.85 GWh). Manufacturing used 9,097.95 GWh, while other industries, grouped under "Other," consumed 9,316 GWh. The electricity sector remained dominant in 2017.

Energy Consumption vs GDP

This graph shows energy consumption per capita vs. GDP across Australian states. The bubble size represents population, and the color represents the state. It is interesting to see that it is likely to lower the consumption if GDP is higher in all states.

Renewable Energy Isotype

The data show Australia's reliance on non-renewable energy, with 205,802 GWh generated from non-renewable sources in 2000, comprising 92% of the total. Renewable energy contributed 17,838 GWh, just 8% of total production. The proportion remained consistent in subsequent years, indicating a low growth rate for renewables.

Comparison of Renewable and Non-Renewable Energy

The non-renewable energy is having less and less portion within the whole energy consumption, leading to more sustainable energy use.
